The story begins with Donald Wanderley, who is driving south with a young girl named Angie, whom he has kidnapped. As he travels through various states, he becomes increasingly agitated and paranoid, hinting at a deeper psychological turmoil. The narrative then shifts to Milburn, New York, where the Chowder Society—composed of four lifelong friends: Frederick "Ricky" Hawthorne, Sears James, John Jaffrey, and Lewis Benedikt—meets to commemorate the first anniversary of the death of their friend Edward Wanderley. Edward died under mysterious circumstances during a party hosted by John, which has left the group haunted by guilt and fear. As the men share ghost stories at their meetings, they begin to experience terrifying nightmares and supernatural occurrences that seem to be connected to their past actions. They decide to invite Don, Edward's nephew and a horror novelist, hoping he can help them confront the horrors they face. Throughout the novel, the Chowder Society members recount their own chilling stories, revealing secrets about their past, including a tragic incident involving a woman named Fenny Bate, who is linked to their shared guilt. As the supernatural elements escalate, the men realize that their past has come back to haunt them, and they must confront the consequences of their actions.
The story culminates in a confrontation with the malevolent force that has been stalking them, forcing the characters to face their deepest fears and the reality of their past misdeeds. Themes Guilt and Consequences: The novel explores how unresolved guilt can manifest in haunting ways, affecting relationships and mental states. Friendship and Isolation: The bonds between the Chowder Society members are tested as they confront their shared history, highlighting the importance of friendship in facing trauma. The Nature of Horror: Straub examines the concept of horror, blending psychological elements with supernatural occurrences, and challenging traditional ghost story tropes. Ghost Story is celebrated for its intricate narrative structure, rich character development, and atmospheric writing. It has received critical acclaim and is considered a significant work in the horror genre, influencing subsequent authors and adaptations, including a film released in 1981.
